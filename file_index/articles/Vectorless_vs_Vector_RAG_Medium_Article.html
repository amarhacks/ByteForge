Vectorless RAG vs. Vector RAG

A technical comparison of embedding-driven retrieval and structure-aware routing, with clear guidance on when each architecture wins.

Abstract Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has become the dominant paradigm for grounding large language models in external knowledge. The prevailing implementation relies on dense vector embeddings and approximate nearest-neighbor search. However, a growing body of practitioners are questioning whether embedding-based retrieval is universally optimal — particularly for structured, hierarchical document corpora. This paper provides a rigorous comparative analysis of Vector RAG and Vectorless RAG: their design principles, algorithmic foundations, retrieval precision trade-offs, and the conditions under which each approach wins.
01

The Problem with "Similar Enough"

Since Lewis et al. (2020) introduced the RAG framework, embedding-based retrieval has become nearly synonymous with knowledge-augmented language generation. The appeal is intuitive: encode text as dense vectors, store them in a vector database, and retrieve the nearest neighbors at query time.

The approach has powered a generation of production AI systems — customer support, legal research, medical informatics, enterprise knowledge management. The engineering ecosystem around it is mature: Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant, FAISS, Chroma. The research ecosystem is deep. It works.

Until it doesn't.

As deployments scale and use cases diversify, a specific failure pattern has become increasingly apparent. Practitioners call it semantic drift: the retrieval system surfaces passages that are semantically adjacent but factually imprecise. A query about "Section 4.2 penalty terms" retrieves any passage about penalties across the entire contract corpus. A query about "Q3 2024 European revenue" retrieves passages about European operations, Q3 performance, and revenue generally — but not the exact figure from the exact section the user needed.

The system found similar text. It did not find the right text.

⚠ The Core Problem

Vector similarity is a proxy for relevance, not a guarantee of it. For unstructured, heterogeneous prose, it is an excellent proxy. For structured documents with precise informational architecture, it is an unreliable one — and the failures are systematically invisible to cosine similarity scores.

This paper argues that the retrieval architecture is not a commodity decision. It is a first-order design choice that directly determines the quality of AI-generated answers — more so, in many deployment contexts, than the choice of language model itself. We examine two paradigms in depth: Vector RAG and Vectorless RAG.

03

Vector RAG: How It Works and Where It Breaks

Vector RAG is currently the dominant paradigm. Its success stems from the power of dense neural embeddings to capture latent semantic relationships between texts that may share no surface-level vocabulary.

The Embedding Foundation

The core of Vector RAG is an embedding model — a neural network trained to map variable-length text into fixed-dimensional dense vectors where semantic similarity correlates with geometric proximity. A query about "cardiac arrest prevention" successfully retrieves passages about "myocardial infarction risk reduction" because both map to nearby regions of the embedding space. Modern models (OpenAI text-embedding-3, Cohere Embed v3, E5, BGE) are trained on massive retrieval benchmarks using contrastive learning: bring semantically related passages together, push unrelated ones apart.

The Chunking Problem (Underappreciated)

A critical and underappreciated design decision in Vector RAG is the chunking strategy — how source documents are divided into indexable units. The choice of chunk size and boundary placement has profound effects on retrieval quality that are not captured by standard embedding benchmarks.

  • Fixed-size chunking — Split every N tokens. Fast to implement, breaks semantic units arbitrarily.
  • Sentence-boundary chunking — Respects linguistic structure, but a sentence often lacks sufficient context to stand alone as a retrievable unit.
  • Semantic chunking — Uses embedding similarity to detect topic shifts. More accurate but expensive and still approximate.
  • Hierarchical (parent-child) — Index detailed chunks, retrieve with parent context. Best precision, highest complexity.

For a legal agreement, a 512-token fixed chunk may split a clause definition from its operative language. A paragraph-boundary chunk may include multiple legally distinct provisions in a single retrievable unit. Neither failure is detectable at indexing time.

Approximate Nearest Neighbor Search

Storing and searching millions of high-dimensional vectors requires ANN algorithms. The most widely deployed are HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World graphs), IVF (Inverted File Indexes), and PQ (Product Quantization). These trade a small degree of recall for dramatic improvements in latency — sub-100ms at 100M+ document scale. This is a genuine engineering achievement, and for most corpus sizes it is not the bottleneck.

Known Failure Modes of Vector RAG

Semantic Drift

Retrieving topically adjacent but factually wrong passages. "Section 4.2 penalty terms" returns any passage mentioning penalties across all documents.

Out-of-Distribution Queries

Embedding models trained on general corpora produce poor representations for highly technical, code-heavy, or domain-specific documents.

Chunk Boundary Artifacts

Critical information split across a chunk boundary is lost or diluted. The model receives half a definition without its operative clause.

Embedding Model Drift

As embedding models improve or change, all stored vectors must be re-indexed. At scale, this is an expensive and operationally complex burden.

04

Vectorless RAG: Navigating Like a Human

Vectorless RAG replaces or substantially reduces reliance on dense vector embeddings in favor of structured navigation of the document corpus. The central insight: many high-value document types have rich inherent structure that a query router can exploit directly — the way a skilled researcher navigates a table of contents before reading the relevant chapter.

The Structural Index

The foundation of vectorless retrieval is a structured index that reflects the actual organization of source documents. Depending on document type, this index takes several forms:

  1. Hierarchical Document Trees — Documents parsed into trees where internal nodes represent sections and leaves represent paragraphs or cells. Section titles and heading levels become navigational metadata.
  2. Knowledge Graphs — Named entities, relationships, and properties extracted from documents and represented as nodes and edges. Retrieval traverses graph paths rather than searching vector spaces.
  3. Inverted Indexes with Structural Metadata — Classic BM25-style indexes augmented with section number, document type, date, regulatory reference, and other structural fields usable for filtering.
  4. Relational Schema Mapping — Semi-structured or tabular documents mapped to database schemas. Queries translated to SQL, returning precise records rather than approximate neighbors.

Query Routing: The Critical Mechanism

Without a universal similarity metric, vectorless RAG must perform more sophisticated query understanding. A query router — a lightweight classifier, LLM, or rule-based system — analyzes each incoming query to determine which section, document type, or graph path most likely contains the answer.

Effective routing involves three sub-tasks: entity extraction (identifying regulation names, product codes, dates), intent classification (is this a definition query, a procedural query, a numerical fact?), and structural pointer resolution (mapping entities and intent to specific index locations).

✓ Where Vectorless Shines

Deterministic precision: A query for "Article 15 subsection 3(b)" deterministically retrieves exactly that subsection — trivial for an inverted index, impossible to guarantee with approximate vector search.

Interpretable retrieval paths: Every retrieval decision is auditable. This is critical for compliance, legal, and regulated applications where "the model retrieved X because of cosine similarity" is not an acceptable audit trail.

No embedding infrastructure: Eliminates embedding models, vector databases, and the associated latency, cost, and maintenance overhead.

Efficient updates: Adding a new document version requires updating the structured index, not re-embedding all changed content.

Knowledge Graph Retrieval: Multi-Hop Power

One of the most powerful forms of vectorless retrieval is knowledge graph navigation. By extracting entities and relationships during indexing, it becomes possible to answer multi-hop queries that require following chains of relationships — for example:

Example Query

"What are the compliance obligations for a Tier 2 supplier under the environmental policy addendum to the master services agreement?"

This requires traversing multiple document sections related through shared entities. Graph traversal handles it naturally. Vector search on individual chunks cannot guarantee it.

The Hard Prerequisite

Vectorless RAG carries a significant prerequisite: it depends critically on the quality and consistency of document structure. Documents that lack consistent heading hierarchies, use idiosyncratic formatting, or mix structured and unstructured content pose serious challenges. The structural parsing pipeline — which must reliably extract headings, entities, relationships, and metadata from heterogeneous formats — is an engineering challenge comparable in complexity to the embedding pipeline it replaces.

Vector RAG finds similar text. Vectorless RAG finds the right place. The best systems know when to do which.

The table reveals a fundamental asymmetry: the two approaches are optimized for different ends of the query precision spectrum. Vector RAG handles semantic diversity in queries at the cost of retrieval precision. Vectorless RAG achieves precision for well-formed queries against well-structured documents at the cost of query flexibility.

The Hallucination Connection

A key motivation for studying retrieval architecture is its downstream effect on language model hallucination. When retrieved passages are irrelevant or only tangentially related, the model faces a difficult inference problem: it must generate an answer despite receiving insufficient grounding, often falling back on parametric knowledge or blending information inappropriately from multiple contexts. Studies on legal QA and enterprise knowledge management have found that improving retrieval precision — holding the language model constant — can reduce hallucination rates by 20–40%. The retrieval architecture is a more important determinant of answer quality than is commonly appreciated.

06

The Emerging Consensus: Hybrid Architectures

The most sophisticated production RAG systems increasingly combine vector and vectorless retrieval in hybrid architectures that exploit the strengths of both paradigms. Three patterns have emerged as particularly effective.

Pattern 1: Two-Stage Retrieval (Structured Filter + Semantic Rerank)

A structured filter stage narrows the candidate set using metadata and structural constraints. A semantic reranking stage then selects the most relevant passages using embedding similarity. Example: a legal AI system first filters all passages to those in Section 8 of contracts dated after 2022 in the relevant jurisdiction — then applies cosine similarity to find the most semantically relevant passage among the filtered set. Result: structural precision plus semantic flexibility.

Pattern 2: Query-Adaptive Routing

A query router analyzes each incoming query and routes it to the appropriate retrieval backend. Queries containing specific identifiers or section references go to the structured index. Open-ended semantic queries go to vector search. Complex multi-hop queries go to the knowledge graph. This requires careful routing logic design and ongoing monitoring for query distribution shifts.

Pattern 3: Sparse-Dense Fusion (RRF)

Sparse-dense fusion combines BM25 (lexical) and dense embedding scores using a weighted combination. Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) is the most commonly used method — hyperparameter-free and robust to score distribution differences between retrieval methods. This provides improved recall across diverse query types with modest additional computational cost, and has become a widely recommended baseline for production RAG systems.

What the Evidence Actually Shows

Controlled empirical comparisons are complicated by the difficulty of constructing equivalent experimental conditions. Nevertheless, several consistent patterns have emerged from research and production deployments.

On Structured Document Benchmarks

Studies comparing retrieval precision on structured document corpora — including SEC filings, legal contracts, and technical standards — consistently find that structured retrieval methods outperform pure vector search on exact-match and fact-extraction tasks. Precision gains of 15–35% have been reported on queries that reference specific sections, dates, or named provisions. These gains are most pronounced for queries that include structural cues ("as defined in Section 3") and less pronounced for semantic reformulations.

On Open-Domain and Semantic Benchmarks

On open-domain QA benchmarks — Natural Questions, TriviaQA, HotpotQA — vector RAG consistently outperforms pure lexical or structured methods. Dense passage retrieval achieves recall@5 scores well above BM25 baselines, particularly for queries requiring semantic generalization. This is where embedding-based retrieval earns its dominant position.

From Enterprise Deployments

Practitioner reports from enterprise AI deployments offer complementary evidence. Teams building RAG systems for policy manuals, product documentation, and compliance databases consistently report that initial deployments using pure vector search surfaced "semantically plausible but wrong" passages. Augmenting with metadata filters and structural routing substantially improved end-user satisfaction metrics — even when automated recall metrics showed only modest improvement. This suggests that tail-case failures are disproportionately visible to users in ways that aggregate benchmarks do not capture.

💡 Key Finding

Retrieval architecture changes that improve automated metrics but degrade human satisfaction — or vice versa — require careful investigation before deployment. Retrieval precision on the wrong distribution of queries is not retrieval precision where it matters.

Step 2: Analyze Your Query Patterns

If user queries are likely to reference specific document locations, use technical identifiers, or require multi-hop reasoning across related sections, structured retrieval delivers substantially better precision. If queries are open-ended, use colloquial language, or need to find information phrased differently across documents, semantic vector search is more appropriate.

Step 3: Evaluate Infrastructure Constraints

Vector RAG requires significant infrastructure: a high-quality embedding model, a production-grade vector database with replication and backup, and an operational pipeline for re-indexing updated documents. Vectorless RAG trades this for investment in structured parsing pipelines and index maintenance. Teams with strong data engineering capabilities but limited ML infrastructure may find vectorless approaches operationally simpler.

Evaluation Methodology

Evaluation of RAG systems must go beyond retrieval recall to measure end-to-end answer quality. We recommend a three-tier framework:

  1. Automated retrieval metrics — Recall@k, Precision@k, MRR on held-out query sets
  2. LLM-as-judge evaluation — Answer faithfulness and relevance rated by a stronger model
  3. Human evaluation — User satisfaction on a representative sample of actual production queries
09

Future Directions

The field of RAG architecture is evolving rapidly, driven by both academic research and production engineering demands. Several directions hold particular promise.

Learned Structural Representations

A natural synthesis of the two paradigms is the development of embedding models that explicitly learn to represent document structure — encoding not just semantic content but positional, relational, and hierarchical information. Document-level contrastive training objectives and structure-aware pre-training strategies are active research areas. If successful, these approaches could yield retrieval systems combining the flexibility of vector search with the structural precision of indexed navigation.

Self-Structuring Documents

Another promising direction involves AI systems that automatically impose structure on poorly structured documents during ingestion. Rather than requiring consistent heading hierarchies, an AI pre-processing stage could infer semantic structure, extract entities and relationships, and construct the navigable index that vectorless RAG requires. This would dramatically expand the applicability of structured retrieval to corpora that currently require vector search by default.

Retrieval-Aware Generation

Most current RAG systems treat retrieval and generation as independent stages. Emerging research on retrieval-aware generation — where the language model's attention mechanism is directly connected to the retrieval index, enabling iterative refinement of both retrieved context and generated text — may render the vector/vectorless distinction less relevant. Systems like RETRO, Self-RAG, IterRetGen, and FLARE are early steps in this direction. The model itself navigates the index during generation rather than receiving a fixed context window.

Conclusion

The debate between vector RAG and vectorless RAG is not a binary choice but a spectrum of architectural possibilities whose optimal point depends on the specific characteristics of the deployment context.

Vector RAG has earned its dominant position through its remarkable ability to handle semantic diversity in queries and its applicability to heterogeneous document corpora without requiring structured parsing. Vectorless RAG has emerged as a compelling alternative for structured, schema-rich domains where retrieval precision is paramount and document structure is consistent enough to support navigable indexing.

The most important insight is that retrieval architecture is not a commodity choice. Teams that treat the retrieval layer as an afterthought and deploy vector search by default on all document types are systematically leaving precision and reliability on the table. Teams that invest in understanding their document corpus and query patterns — and design retrieval architectures accordingly — achieve substantially better end-user outcomes.

The emerging consensus points toward hybrid architectures that combine structural filtering, lexical matching, and semantic similarity in query-adaptive pipelines. As document AI matures, we expect the boundaries between these approaches to blur further, with unified retrieval frameworks that learn to exploit whatever structural and semantic signals are present in the corpus.

The future of RAG is not embeddings or structure — it is both, intelligently composed, each where it belongs.
